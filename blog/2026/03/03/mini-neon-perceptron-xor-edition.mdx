The [neon perceptron](/blog/2025/12/11/neon-perceptron-digital-twin/) is coming
along, but before we commit to the full 5×5 input build with custom PCBs and
nine hidden neurons and seven-segment displays, we're building a smaller one
first. A 4-input, 3-hidden, 2-output mini version---just enough to be
interesting, and just small enough that we can actually wire it up and debug the
thing without losing our minds.

The framing for this smaller version is
[XOR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclusive_or), which turns out to be a
surprisingly perfect fit.

## Why XOR matters

In 1969, Marvin Minsky and Seymour Papert published
[_Perceptrons_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perceptrons_%28book%29)---a
mathematical analysis of what
[Frank Rosenblatt's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Rosenblatt)
single-layer perceptron could and couldn't do. The headline result was that a
single-layer perceptron can't compute XOR. It can do AND, it can do OR, but it
can't learn to output "true" when its inputs _disagree_. The function isn't
linearly separable, and that's that.

This contributed to the first
[AI winter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AI_winter)---a long period where
funding and enthusiasm for neural network research dried up. The fix, of course,
was to add more layers. A multi-layer perceptron with a hidden layer can solve
XOR just fine. But it took until the 1980s for backpropagation to make training
these deeper networks practical, and for the field to recover.

So XOR is _the_ classic demonstration of why multi-layer networks matter. And
that's exactly what the mini neon perceptron is: a physical, glowing
multi-layer perceptron that you can watch solve (or attempt to solve) XOR in
real time.

## The setup

The mini perceptron has a 2×2 input grid---four pixels. The task is diagonal
detection: light up one diagonal (top-left and bottom-right) and the network
should activate one output; light up the other diagonal (top-right and
bottom-left) and it should activate the other. This is a 2D XOR
problem---the network needs to distinguish between the two diagonals, which
requires looking at the _combination_ of inputs rather than any single pixel.

The two output nodes represent the two diagonals. Whichever has the higher
activation gets a glowing halo---that's the network's answer.

Between input and output sit three hidden neurons with tanh activation. The
output layer uses softmax, so the two outputs sum to 1 and you can read them as
a confidence distribution.

## 18 weights, 18 potentiometers

The network has 12 weights from input to hidden (4×3) and 6 from hidden to
output (3×2). That's 18 weights total.

Right now they're randomly initialised, so the network won't actually detect
diagonals---it'll just produce arbitrary outputs. But 18 is a small enough
number that in the physical version, each weight will be a potentiometer that
you can manually adjust[^pots]. Sit down, light up a diagonal, and twiddle the
knobs until the right output lights up. Then check the other diagonal. Then
check the non-diagonal patterns. Keep going until it works for all inputs.

[^pots]:
    This is how Rosenblatt's original perceptron worked too, albeit with
    motorised potentiometers rather than manual ones. We're going even more
    old-school.

That's training a neural network by hand. No backpropagation, no gradient
descent---just a human in the loop, adjusting weights and watching the
activations change. It's slow and probably frustrating, but it makes the
relationship between weights and behaviour viscerally obvious in a way that
staring at a loss curve never will.

## What's next

This mini version is a prototype for the
[full neon perceptron](https://github.com/ANUcybernetics/neon-perceptron)---a
chance to test the physical design (flexible LEDs as wires, potentiometers for
weights, the overall form factor) before scaling up to the
[5×5 / 9-hidden / 10-output version](/blog/2025/12/11/neon-perceptron-digital-twin/).
If the mini version works and looks good, we'll know the concept holds up. If
it doesn't, better to find out with 18 weights than 315.
