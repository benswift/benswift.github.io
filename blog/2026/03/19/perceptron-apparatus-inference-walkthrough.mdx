The perceptron apparatus is a 1.2-metre diameter circular wooden table that
classifies handwritten digits using concentric rings of sliders and a
logarithmic slide rule. It's a piece of speculative design from the
[School of Cybernetics](https://cybernetics.anu.edu.au) at ANU, imagined as an
artefact from a world where the knowledge to build digital computers has been
lost, but the mathematics of neural networks survived. So people built _this_
instead.

The apparatus implements a real trained
[multilayer perceptron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilayer_perceptron)---a
36→6→10 MLP that takes a 6×6 pixel image of a digit, runs a forward pass through
its weights and activations, and produces a prediction. Every multiply is a
slide rule operation. Every accumulation is a slider moving along a track. The
whole inference process is physically legible, each step visible to anyone
standing around the table.

The physical table has been built---fabricated by Sam Shellard at UC's
[Workshop7](https://www.canberra.edu.au/future-students/study-at-uc/study-areas/design/workshop7),
with all the laser cutting and CNC routing files available in the
[GitHub repo](https://github.com/ANUcybernetics/perceptron-apparatus). This post
walks through the inference process with an interactive digital twin of the
apparatus. If you've seen the
[neon perceptron](/blog/2025/12/11/neon-perceptron-digital-twin/) posts, this is
its older, more analogue sibling.

## The architecture

The apparatus has five concentric rings, each corresponding to a layer of the
network:

- **Ring A** (outermost): 36 radial sliders for the input pixels---one per cell
  in the 6×6 grid
- **Ring B**: weight sliders for the input→hidden connections (36×6 = 216
  weights)
- **Ring C**: 6 radial sliders for the hidden neuron activations
- **Ring D**: weight sliders for the hidden→output connections (6×10 = 60
  weights)
- **Ring E** (innermost): 10 radial sliders for the output---one per digit class

Around the outside sit two additional rings: a logarithmic rule ring for
performing multiplication, and a
[ReLU](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifier_(neural_networks)>) reference
ring for reading off the activation function. To multiply two numbers you rotate
the log ring to align the two values, then read the product off the outer
scale---the same principle as a
[slide rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slide_rule), wrapped into a circle.

The forward pass is:

```
hidden = relu(input × B)
output = hidden × D
prediction = argmax(output)
```

No bias terms, no softmax on the output. The hidden layer uses
[ReLU](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifier_(neural_networks)>)---negative
values snap to zero, which on the apparatus just means the slider stays at the
bottom of its track.

## Try it

Pick a digit from the thumbnails below and hit "Step through" to watch the
forward pass animate on the apparatus. Each step computes one hidden or output
neuron---accumulating the weighted inputs, applying ReLU where appropriate, and
sliding the result into place. "Instant" skips the animation and shows the final
state.

<ApparatusInference client:only="svelte" />

The weights are real---trained on
[MNIST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MNIST_database) via
[Axon](https://hexdocs.pm/axon/Axon.html) in Elixir, then exported to the
digital twin. The five sample digits (0, 1, 4, 6, 7) are ones the model
classifies correctly. With only 276 parameters and aggressive downsampling from
28×28 to 6×6 pixels, it's not going to win any accuracy benchmarks---but that
was never the point. The apparatus exists to make the forward pass _visible_.
