The perceptron apparatus is a 1.2-metre diameter circular wooden table that
classifies handwritten digits using concentric rings of sliders and a
logarithmic slide rule. It's a piece of speculative design from the
[School of Cybernetics](https://cybernetics.anu.edu.au) at ANU, imagined as an
artefact from the Museum of the Future: a world where the knowledge to build
digital computers has been lost, but the mathematics of neural networks
survived. So people built _this_ instead.

The apparatus implements a real trained
[multilayer perceptron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilayer_perceptron)---a
36→6→10 MLP that takes a 6×6 pixel image of a digit, runs a forward pass through
its weights and activations, and produces a prediction. Every multiply is a
slide rule operation. Every accumulation is a slider moving along a track. The
whole inference process is physically legible, each step visible to anyone
standing around the table.

This post walks through that process with an interactive digital twin of the
apparatus. If you've seen the
[neon perceptron](/blog/2025/12/11/neon-perceptron-digital-twin/) posts, this is
its older, more analogue sibling.

## The architecture

The apparatus has five concentric rings, each corresponding to a layer of the
network:

- **Ring A** (outermost): 36 radial sliders for the input pixels---one per cell
  in the 6×6 grid
- **Ring B**: weight sliders for the input→hidden connections (36×6 = 216
  weights)
- **Ring C**: 6 radial sliders for the hidden neuron activations
- **Ring D**: weight sliders for the hidden→output connections (6×10 = 60
  weights)
- **Ring E** (innermost): 10 radial sliders for the output---one per digit class

Around the outside sits a logarithmic rule ring for performing multiplication.
To multiply two numbers you rotate the inner ring to align values, then read the
product off the outer scale. It's the same principle as a
[slide rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slide_rule), wrapped into a circle.

The forward pass is:

```
hidden = relu(input × B)
output = hidden × D
prediction = argmax(output)
```

No bias terms, no softmax on the output. The hidden layer uses
[ReLU](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectifier_(neural_networks)>)---negative
values snap to zero, which on the apparatus just means the slider stays at the
bottom of its track.

The weights are real---trained on

## 57% accuracy (and why that's fine)

The model gets about 57% accuracy overall. It can't classify digits 2, 3, 5, 8,
or 9 at all---they never come out on top. This is what happens when you
aggressively
[downsample 28×28 images to 6×6 pixels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MNIST_database),
use only 6 hidden neurons with no bias terms, and train for a handful of epochs.
State-of-the-art MNIST models hit 99.8% with millions of parameters. This one
has 276.

But accuracy isn't the point. The apparatus exists to make neural network
inference _visible_---to turn an opaque matrix multiplication into something you
can watch, touch, and reason about. A model that gets everything right would be
less interesting to demonstrate than one that sometimes fails, because the
failures are where you start asking "why did it think that was a 4?" and tracing
the answer back through the rings.

## The slide rule trick

The key mechanical insight is that you can do multiplication with addition if
you work in log space. On the apparatus, ring B and ring D hold weight values as
positions on a logarithmic scale. To multiply an input activation by a weight,
you rotate the rule ring to align the two values, then read the product where
they intersect. It's slow---276 individual multiplications for a single forward
pass---but it's transparent in a way that a GPU never will be.

The accumulation step (summing all the products into a single neuron activation)
is even simpler: each product nudges a slider along its track, and the final
position _is_ the sum. ReLU is just "if the slider is below zero, leave it at
zero". The whole thing is mechanical.

## It's just arithmetic

We've gotten very good at treating neural networks as black boxes---data goes
in, predictions come out, and the middle is someone else's problem. The
apparatus forces you to confront every single multiply-accumulate, to feel the
_tedium_ of a forward pass, and to appreciate the sheer grunt work that modern hardware hides from us.

Neural networks are arithmetic. Fancy, heavily optimised, massively parallelised
arithmetic---but arithmetic nonetheless. If you can do it with a wooden table
and some sliders, it stops feeling like magic.
